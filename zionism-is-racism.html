UN slogan / racism charge / legitimacy attack

Zionism Is Racism

The phrase revives the UN charge made famous by Resolution 3379 and treats Jewish self-determination as racism by definition.

DEFINITION

“Zionism is racism” is the claim that Zionism is itself a form of racial discrimination. It does not only accuse Israel of a bad law, policy, border, or war. It defines the Jewish movement for self-determination in the land of Israel as racist at its root.

That is why the phrase is stronger than ordinary criticism of Israel. It begins with a verdict on Zionism itself. Before any evidence is weighed, the Jewish claim to a homeland is treated as a racial offense.

In the glossary sense, the term is not a neutral description. It is a totalizing label. It takes the word Zionism, which can cover many Jewish arguments for safety, return, statehood, and refuge, and reduces it to one alleged essence: racism.

COMMON USAGE

The phrase appears in protest signs, boycott campaigns, campus activism, UN debates, NGO statements, and antizionist writing. It usually works as a shortcut. Instead of naming a specific Israeli act, it names Zionism as the wrong.

The slogan became famous after United Nations General Assembly Resolution 3379. On November 10, 1975, the General Assembly declared “that zionism is a form of racism and racial discrimination.” The resolution passed by 72 votes to 35, with 32 abstentions. The UN later revoked it, but the phrase remained in use.

In current use, the phrase often appears without the UN history attached. A sign, chant, or post may repeat it as if it were settled fact, while leaving out that the same UN later repealed the resolution and that the original vote came during a Cold War alignment hostile to Israel.

IMPLIED MEANING

The phrase implies that Jewish self-determination is not like other forms of peoplehood. It says the Jewish state is based on racial exclusion by definition.

That is the key move. A person can criticize Israeli governments, courts, laws, wars, settlements, military conduct, or treatment of minorities without denying that Jews are a people with ties to the land of Israel. “Zionism is racism” goes further. It says the Jewish claim itself is the problem.

Its implied comparison is also uneven. Movements for Arab, Kurdish, Armenian, Ukrainian, or Palestinian self-determination are usually judged by conduct, borders, and rights. The slogan asks that Jewish self-determination be judged as illegitimate before those questions begin.

ORIGIN / LINEAGE

The argument did not begin at the UN in 1975. One important source was Fayez A. Sayegh’s 1965 pamphlet Zionist Colonialism in Palestine, published in Beirut by the Research Center of the Palestine Liberation Organization. Sayegh argued that Zionism was a colonial movement built on separation, expansion, and racial exclusion. His work helped give later antizionist campaigns a simple claim: Zionism was not Jewish self-determination, but racism.

By 1975, that claim had moved through several international forums. Resolution 3379 cited the Mexico City World Conference of International Women’s Year, held from June 19 to July 2, 1975; the Organization of African Unity meeting in Kampala, held from July 28 to August 1, 1975; and the Non-Aligned foreign ministers’ conference in Lima, held from August 25 to 30, 1975. In those settings, Zionism was linked with colonialism, apartheid, foreign occupation, and racial discrimination.

The UN Third Committee approved the draft on October 17, 1975, by 70 votes to 29, with 27 abstentions. The General Assembly adopted Resolution 3379 on November 10, 1975, by 72 votes to 35, with 32 abstentions. Support came from Arab states, the Soviet bloc, and several non-aligned states. Benin and Kuwait were among the named sponsors. Other supporters included Algeria, Egypt, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, Somalia, Syria, Cuba, Yugoslavia, and the PLO observer.

Israel’s ambassador, Chaim Herzog, denounced the resolution at the UN. The American ambassador, Daniel Patrick Moynihan, rejected it for the United States. On December 16, 1991, the General Assembly revoked Resolution 3379 through Resolution 46/86. The repeal passed by 111 votes to 25, with 13 abstentions.

The repeal did not erase the phrase because Resolution 3379 had already done its work outside the UN. It gave campaigners a ready citation, a date, and a formal sentence from an international body. Even after 1991, the slogan could be used as if the old verdict still carried authority.

FUNCTION

The function of the phrase is to make the conclusion arrive first. If Zionism is racism, then Israel does not need to be judged by the same tests applied to other states. Its Jewish character is already treated as proof of guilt.

This is why the phrase has lasted. It turns many separate questions into one charge. Borders, refugees, settlements, security, minority rights, war, and citizenship can all be discussed as evidence of one prior claim: that Jewish self-determination is racist by nature.

The phrase also shortens argument. It lets the speaker move from an Israeli act to the condemnation of Zionism as such, then from Zionism to the condemnation of Israel’s existence as a Jewish state. That movement is why the phrase often functions as more than protest language.

NOTES

There are serious debates about Israel’s policies and actions. These include the status of the West Bank, Gaza, East Jerusalem, settlements, refugees, military law, Arab citizens of Israel, and the rights of Jews and Arabs in the land. Those debates require evidence.

“Zionism is racism” often avoids that work. It treats Zionism itself as the answer. The phrase also reverses the history of Zionism. Zionism arose from Jewish exile, persecution, attachment to the land of Israel, and the need for Jewish self-rule. It was not founded as a doctrine of racial supremacy. The slogan recasts that history as racial domination.

A careful use of language matters here. Calling a law discriminatory, a military action unlawful, or a government decision unjust is not the same as calling Zionism racism. The first kind of claim can be tested. The second often turns a dispute over facts into a verdict on Jewish peoplehood.

RESPONSES / CRITICISMS

The main criticism is that the phrase applies a rule to Jews that is not applied in the same way to other peoples. Many countries have founding peoples, official languages, state religions, homeland laws, or immigration rules for a diaspora. These may be criticized. They do not usually lead to the claim that the country has no right to exist.

A second criticism is that the phrase blurs two different ideas. Racism means racial hierarchy or discrimination. Zionism means Jewish self-determination. Treating them as the same makes honest argument harder.

The deepest objection is that the slogan turns the Jewish people’s return to self-rule into a racial crime. It does not merely criticize Israel. It condemns the Jewish claim before any specific case is heard.

A direct response should separate the questions. Does a specific law discriminate? Did a specific act violate rights? Does a state feature resemble practices used elsewhere? Those questions can be argued with evidence. The slogan prevents that separation by making Zionism itself the charge.
